The Wood Duck and the Hollow in the Tree

A drake in iridescent green, a hen that climbs thirty feet up a sycamore, and one of the quieter conservation wins of the last century.

The Wood Duck (Aix sponsa) drake wears one of the most elaborate plumages of any waterfowl on the continent — an iridescent green helm crossed with violet, a white chinstrap, a russet breast flecked with pale spots, and an eye the color of arterial blood. He is the bird most birders look for first along a wooded creek in spring, and the one most often painted into field-guide plates. On Earth Day, he is a useful reminder of how much of the living world depends on details we almost never notice — like a hollow thirty feet up a sycamore.

Wood Ducks nest in trees. Hens lay between nine and fourteen eggs in cavities high above the forest floor — old pileated woodpecker holes, broken limbs, sometimes nest boxes where a swamp has been logged through. Within twenty-four hours of hatching, the ducklings climb to the rim and jump. They land on leaf litter, occasionally on water, and bounce — small, hollow-boned, almost weightless. Nothing about that drop is gentle, and yet the survival rate is high. The hen calls them out one by one with a soft kuk-kuk-kuk from below.

The drake's voice is more memorable than his plumage suggests. He gives a thin rising whistle — jeeeeb — that carries through cypress understory long before you see him. Females respond with a sharper oo-eek when they flush. In flight, the wings do not whistle the way a goldeneye's do; the call is what tells you what just left the slough.

Aix sponsa is the only widespread tree-nesting duck in North America. The Hooded Merganser shares the habit but at much lower density. The species ranges from southern Canada down through the wooded eastern United States, with a separate Pacific population in the river bottoms of California, Oregon, and Washington. They prefer slow black water — beaver ponds, oxbows, swamp edges — anywhere with tannic shallows, abundant acorns, and a tree old enough to have rotted out a chamber.

By 1900 the Wood Duck was nearly gone. Market hunting and the felling of mature bottomland forests had pushed the population to a fragment of what it had been. The Migratory Bird Treaty Act of 1918 closed the season, and by mid-century, conservationists were nailing wooden nest boxes to trees across the eastern seaboard. The recovery is one of the quieter wins of the last century. Today the species is one of the most numerous breeding waterfowl in the eastern flyway. You can stand on a boardwalk in a Virginia state park in April and watch four pairs work the same beaver pond.

If you have never seen one, look upward in a flooded forest at dawn. The drakes spend more time perched in trees than most people expect — branches twenty feet over the water, head tucked, a glint of green and white where a duck has no business being.
