The Peacock's Train Is Not a Tail

On Earth Day, a closer look at one of the species humans have lived next to longest.

The Indian Peafowl (Pavo cristatus) male — the peacock — carries one of the largest ornamental structures in the bird world. It is also one of the species humans have lived alongside longest, which makes it a good one to sit with on Earth Day. What looks like an enormous tail is actually a train of elongated upper tail coverts, fanned and held up by the much shorter true tail beneath. Each eye-spot in the train is not painted with pigment but built from microscopic lattices of keratin that bend light into iridescent blues, golds, and greens. The color shifts as he moves. This is structural color — the same trick a hummingbird's gorget pulls off, scaled up to a hundred and fifty feathers.

A mature train can stretch six feet and weigh nearly a kilogram. The bird drags it through dust and brush all year, then in the spring he raises it. The display is a slow rattle: he turns slightly to the female, vibrates the quills with a sound like a soft purr, and lets the eye-spots tremble. Studies of the courtship have shown that hens watch the lower rows of eye-spots more than the upper — the part of the train closest to the male's body, where symmetry is hardest to fake.

Darwin wrote, in a letter to a friend, that the sight of a peacock's tail made him sick. The bird was a problem for natural selection — every gram of train a liability against predators, every shimmer a beacon. He spent the rest of his life building the case for sexual selection partly to account for it. The eye-spots remain one of the most-studied ornaments in evolutionary biology, and the answer is still not entirely settled. Some hens seem to prefer larger trains, some denser ocelli, some are indifferent. The display works on more than one channel.

There is more going on than the eye picks up. The peacock's vibration produces infrasound — pulses below human hearing — that may carry body size and condition across distance. The crest of bare-shafted feathers on his head is tuned to that same frequency band, and resonates when another male displays nearby. He listens with his head.

Indian Peafowl are native to the Indian subcontinent and Sri Lanka. They are omnivorous and adaptable — they eat grain, lizards, snakes, scorpions, fallen fruit. In the foothills of the Western Ghats and in the dry forests of central India, the peacock's call carries for almost a kilometer. It is a single drawn-out wail, repeated, often before rain. Farmers read the call as a weather forecast.

The hen — the peahen — is muted brown, smaller, with a short fan of green at the neck and the same crest as the male. She does the building. Indian Peafowl nest on the ground, in scrapes lined with grass and leaves under thick brush. She sits the clutch alone for twenty-eight days. The chicks fledge fast and follow her for months, learning where the water is and what the alarm calls of langurs and chital deer mean.
