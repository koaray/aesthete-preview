The Peacock's Train Is Not a Tail

A correction about Pavo cristatus, and what those hundred and fifty eye-spots actually are.

This week falls on Earth Day, and we are spending it with the Indian Peafowl (Pavo cristatus) — one of the more familiar birds in the world, and one of the more misunderstood. The fan you see when a peacock raises his feathers is not his tail. It is a structure of elongated upper tail coverts, feathers that grow from above the actual tail. The tail itself sits hidden beneath the train, short and brown and unremarkable.

That distinction matters more than it sounds. The covert feathers carry up to a hundred and fifty eye-spots, called ocelli, and the color in each one is not pigment. It is light. The barbules along the feather are structured at the nanometer scale, layered with melanin rods that act as a two-dimensional photonic crystal. Light hits, scatters, interferes with itself, and what returns to your eye is iridescent green, blue, and copper that shifts as the bird turns. Grind a feather to powder and it is brown. Worn intact on the back of the bird, it shimmers because of geometry.

When a peacock displays, he raises the train into a fan over his back, lowers his head, and begins what ornithologists call train-rattling — a high-frequency vibration of the quills that produces an infrasonic component below human hearing. The eye-spots flicker. The peahen, who is brown and small and has no train at all, watches. Recent work suggests she pays particular attention to the lower rows of ocelli, the ones nearest his body, rather than the topmost feathers that catch our eye when we look at a peacock from the side. There is more we have wrong about what she is choosing than we have right.

The bird is native to the Indian subcontinent, where it has lived in dry woodland and forest edge for as long as anyone has been watching. It roosts in trees at night despite spending most of its day on the ground. It eats grain, fruit, insects, lizards, and small snakes — there is an old account of a peacock killing a young cobra, which is part of why the bird is sacred in Hindu tradition and why a peacock feather appears in iconography of Krishna. The voice is a piercing two-note call, most often heard at dawn and at the approach of rain. People who keep peafowl in temperate climates know it as the sound that travels two miles in still air and ends conversations in adjacent yards.

The males molt the train every year. By midsummer the eye-spots are dropping from his back and he is, briefly, an ordinary bird. He regrows the whole structure over winter. The work of producing those coverts — with their structured barbules and their iridescence, and a length that can reach five feet in a mature male — is one of the larger annual investments any bird makes in display. He makes it again, and again, and again.

The painting on this pocket is of an adult Pavo cristatus, painted by hand at the size you see it on the shirt. Each ocellus is placed one at a time. Stand near a flock at dawn in spring and you will hear the call before you see the bird — a two-note cry that carries two miles in still air, then a long pause, then the call again.
