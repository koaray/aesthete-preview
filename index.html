The Hen Wood Duck and the Other Half of Spring

For Earth Day, the bird most birders walk past on their way to the drake.

The Wood Duck (Aix sponsa) is one of the most photographed ducks on the continent — but almost always the drake. The hen, less flamboyant than her partner, gets walked past. This week, for Earth Day, we want to give her the field tag.

She is dappled gray-brown across the back, with a soft trailing crest and a clean white teardrop ring around the eye that looks deliberately drawn. Her call — a rising oo-EEK — is the sound most often heard from a wood-edged pond at dusk, and is one of the few ways to confirm Wood Ducks are present at all when the light goes flat and the drake disappears into the snag line.

She is also the architect of the next generation. The hen, not the drake, chooses the nest cavity. She inspects holes thirty or forty feet up in dead trees, sometimes investigating dozens before committing. She lines the bottom with down plucked from her own breast. She lays one egg per day for nearly two weeks, and only after the clutch is complete does she begin to incubate. The drake is, by then, mostly background.

Within a day of hatching, she calls her ducklings out of the cavity. They climb to the entrance and leap — and they do not glide. They fall, sometimes thirty feet, bounce, right themselves, and follow her to water. The drake is rarely present for this. By the time the eggs hatch, he has typically dispersed.

There is a behavior in Wood Duck hens called brood parasitism, or egg dumping. A hen will sometimes lay one or two eggs in another hen's cavity before settling on her own. The receiving hen incubates the additional eggs without distinction. Some nests end up holding twenty or thirty eggs from multiple mothers. It is one of the few cases in North American waterfowl where the social structure of nesting is more cooperative than competitive.

Hens show strong natal philopatry — they return year after year to nest near where they themselves hatched. A particular cavity above a particular pond may carry the same lineage for decades. Remove the dead tree, and you remove the line. This is the part most easily missed: the bird needs the snag, and the snag needs to be left standing.

For Earth Day, the easy story is the species you already know to look for. The harder story is the one beside it. The drake gets the photographs and the framed prints and the field-guide cover. The hen does the architecture. She picks the cavity, lays the eggs, calls the ducklings down, and shepherds them across open water in the half-light. Earth Day, at the simplest, is an argument for paying attention to the parts of the living world that do not advertise themselves. The hen Wood Duck is a study in that.

We painted her this week from observation — perched on a leaning snag at the edge of a beaver pond in early spring, looking down. The pocket tee is cropped from the original, and ships with a numbered field tag: kingdom Animalia, phylum Chordata, class Aves, order Anseriformes, family Anatidae, genus Aix, species sponsa. The taxonomy is the inheritance she carries. The dead tree is the reason she still carries it.
