The Wood Duck Drake in Full Spring Plumage

A North American duck that nests thirty feet up in trees, and a drake whose breeding dress reads like an accident of overdesign.

The Wood Duck (Aix sponsa) drake is the centerpiece of this week's tee, and on the week of Earth Day it feels right to begin with him — a bird native to the wooded ponds and slow rivers of North America, painted from observation in his April best.

His plumage is hard to describe without overshooting. The head is iridescent green and purple, banded by two thin white lines that look deliberately drawn. Eyes the color of arterial blood. A white chinstrap. A russet chest stippled with white. Buff flanks held against blacker undertones. Stand him at the edge of a pond in low side-light and you will see at least three colors the field guide cannot name.

He earns it. Wood Ducks are one of very few North American waterfowl that nest in tree cavities — old woodpecker holes, broken-off limbs, sometimes nest boxes nailed to a snag along a beaver pond. The hen — quieter, dressed in soft gray-brown with a white teardrop ring around the eye — does the choosing. The drake follows. The competition for those cavities is real, and his plumage is the argument he makes.

Once the eggs are laid, things get strange. The hen sits on a clutch of ten or twelve eggs thirty feet up. The drake stays nearby for the first weeks of incubation, then drifts off to molt with the other males into a duller eclipse plumage. When the ducklings hatch — and this is the part that does not stop being remarkable — the hen calls them up to the rim of the cavity, and they jump. Day-old ducklings, the size of a walnut, drop thirty feet to the leaf litter or the water below, bounce, and follow their mother to the nearest pond. They do not glide. They do not slow themselves. They simply fall.

It is not the only thing that makes Wood Ducks worth watching, but it is the thing you remember.

By midsummer the drake's spring colors are gone. He wears the eclipse plumage — drab brown, bill faded, almost indistinguishable from the hen — until late fall, when he molts back into the dress on this tee. The cycle holds. By February in the Carolinas and March in New England, the ponds are full of paired birds again, and the drakes are doing the slow neck-stretching displays that lead to next year's cavity.

The painting on the pocket is the drake at that peak — late winter into spring, before pair bonds break and the eclipse begins. It was painted at the size of the pocket, in Procreate on iPad, from observation rather than from a photograph. The studio has stood under the trees and watched these birds. The white teardrop on the hen, the brushed look of the drake's flank — those details did not come from a reference shot.

Wood Ducks were nearly gone from the eastern United States by 1900, hunted hard and short on cavities. Nest boxes and stricter seasons brought them back. They are now one of the most common breeding ducks east of the Mississippi.

Stand at the edge of a wooded pond in April. You will hear the hen first — a thin rising whistle, almost questioning — from somewhere up in the canopy.
