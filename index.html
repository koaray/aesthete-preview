The Wood Duck and the Cavity Tree

A duck that climbs, nests thirty feet up, and wears one of the most extravagant coats on the continent.

The Wood Duck (Aix sponsa) drake is one of the hardest birds on the continent to look away from. Iridescent green helm combed into a crest, a clean white chinstrap, russet breast flecked with small white teardrops, flanks the color of buff leather. His eye is arterial red. On a cold slough at first light, he sits motionless on a submerged log and looks more like an heirloom than a living thing.

Earth Day passed this week — a useful moment to remember that the Wood Duck was nearly shot out of North America by 1918, then brought back through cavity nest boxes, hunting limits, and the Migratory Bird Treaty. The recovery is one of the quieter wins of the last century.

Wood Ducks are among the only North American ducks that nest in trees. The hen, more soberly plumed in gray-brown with a white teardrop ring around the eye, seeks out a cavity thirty feet up — an old pileated woodpecker hole, a lightning scar, a hollow in a cottonwood or a sycamore over water. She lays ten to fifteen eggs in the wood dust at the bottom. There is no lining, no down, until she plucks her own breast feathers later.

Within twenty-four hours of hatching, the ducklings have to come down. The hen stands at the cavity rim and calls — a thin kuk-kuk-kuk up into the dark of the hollow — and one by one the ducklings climb to the lip with their claws (they have claws), stand on the edge for a second, and jump. They fall the full thirty feet and bounce on the forest floor, unhurt. Then they follow her, on foot, to water.

This is routine. It happens every spring across the forested wetlands of North America, in the same quiet undrama as frogs returning to a pond. Few people see it. The hen's call is soft, the ducklings move at dawn, and the tree is never a place you'd think to look up into.

The drake's plumage, extravagant as it is, is a spring costume. He molts into a drab eclipse coat by mid-summer, nearly female-colored, and spends the rest of the year quiet. The green helm is for a few months only — for courtship, for the display on still water in late winter when pairs form. If you want to see him at his full weight of color, go out between February and May, on any slow river with overhanging oaks, at the hour when the light is still coming through sideways.

The pocket on this tee is the drake in full courtship plumage. Painted by hand, at the size of the pocket, in Procreate — layer over layer, green over black over russet, with the white teardrops on the breast done last. The species was chosen because it is one of the quiet astonishments of North American wildlife: a duck that climbs, a duck that nests like an owl, a bird so ornamental that the first European painters of the continent assumed it was an import.

Stand under the trees along any wooded slough in April. Look up. Listen for a soft whistling overhead. The Wood Duck passes through the canopy faster than you'd believe a duck could move.
