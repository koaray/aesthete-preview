The Wood Duck Nests in Trees

Aix sponsa — the only North American duck that climbs to the canopy to lay her eggs.

The Wood Duck (Aix sponsa) is one of the only North American ducks that nests in trees. Earth Day week, and we are writing about a bird that does its own arguing.

The hen finds a cavity thirty or forty feet up in a sycamore or a black gum, often a hole left by a pileated woodpecker, and lays a clutch of nine to fifteen creamy eggs on a bed of down she has plucked from her own breast. Within a day of hatching, the ducklings leap.

There is no easing into it. The hen flies down, lands at the base of the tree, and calls upward in a soft kuk-kuk-kuk. The ducklings climb to the lip of the cavity using sharp claws on their webbed feet — equipment built for exactly this moment — and step off. They tumble down through air, hit the leaf litter, and bounce. Most are unhurt. Within minutes the brood is following the hen toward water, sometimes a quarter mile through the woods.

The drake, by then, has often left. Wood Duck pairs bond on the wintering grounds and the male escorts the female back to the nest tree, but he plays no role in raising the brood. What he does instead is wear what may be the most elaborate plumage of any waterfowl on the continent: an iridescent green and purple helm slicked back over the crown, a white chinstrap, a russet breast freckled with white, sides of warm tan, eyes the color of arterial blood. In summer he molts this all away and goes drab — eclipse plumage — and for a few weeks looks almost like the hen, who is herself plain in the way good camouflage requires: gray-brown overall, with a teardrop of white around the eye.

You see Wood Ducks where you do not expect to see ducks. They like wooded swamps, beaver ponds, the slow elbow of a creek where willows lean over the water. They eat acorns. A flushed pair will burst up through the canopy in a way that startles you, calling oo-eek-oo-eek as they go.

The species was nearly gone by 1900 — hunted hard, squeezed by the loss of bottomland hardwood forest. It came back because people built nest boxes, wrote new laws, gave the bird room. The recovery is one of the quieter wins of the last century. The hen does not know any of it. She calls down from the cavity. The ducklings step into the air.
