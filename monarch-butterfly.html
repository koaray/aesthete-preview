Three Thousand Miles to a Forest They've Never Seen

The only insect that makes a true two-way migration — and the generation built to make it.

The Monarch Butterfly (Danaus plexippus) is the only insect known to perform a true two-way migration. Each fall, the generation that hatches in late summer in Canada and the upper Midwest sets off for a handful of oyamel fir groves in the mountains of central Mexico — three thousand miles south, to a forest no individual butterfly has ever seen. Earth Day arrives as the return is underway, the southbound generation's grandchildren now fanning back across the continent in three or four overlapping waves.

The migrating generation is biologically distinct from the ones that came before it. Where summer monarchs live four or five weeks and breed continuously, the fall generation enters reproductive diapause and lives eight times longer. They do not mate until spring. They are built to travel.

How they navigate is a piece of biology that took decades to work out. Their antennae contain light-sensitive proteins called cryptochromes that function as a sun compass, calibrated against an internal clock so that the angle of the sun is read accurately throughout the day. Strip the antennae and the butterflies still fly — they just fly in random directions.

The Mexican overwintering sites themselves were not described in the scientific literature until 1975. For most of the twentieth century, no one in the United States or Canada knew where the butterflies went. Local communities in Michoacán knew. They had watched the orange tides arrive each November for as long as anyone could remember, settling onto the same firs in clusters so dense the branches sagged.

The caterpillar lives on a single plant family. Milkweed (Asclepias) contains cardiac glycosides — compounds that disrupt vertebrate heart muscle — and the larva sequesters them in its tissues without harm. The toxin carries through metamorphosis. The orange-and-black wings of the adult are an honest advertisement. A bird that eats one monarch usually does not eat a second.

Females test milkweed with their feet before laying. Chemoreceptors on the tarsi sample the leaf surface; if the species is wrong, or the plant is in poor condition, she moves on. A single female may visit dozens of plants in an afternoon and lay only a few eggs on each one.

What is on the tee is the adult — wings spread, the four orange panels divided by black veins, the white-spotted margins. Painted at pocket scale.

Monarch numbers have declined steeply over the last three decades. The reasons are several and well documented: loss of milkweed across the corn-and-soybean belt, deforestation at the Mexican wintering grounds, severe weather, neonicotinoid use on adjacent crops. The eastern population is still measured each winter by the area of forest its colonies occupy in Michoacán, a strange unit — hectares of butterfly. Some winters it is a few. Some winters it is more.

In the spring fields of Texas and Oklahoma right now, the first females of the returning generation are laying eggs on the new milkweed shoots. Watch the underside of the leaves for a single pale dome the size of a pinhead. That is where the next generation begins.
