The Monarch Butterfly and the Forest It Has Never Seen

Each fall, three thousand miles to a grove of oyamel fir in central Mexico — a place none of the migrating butterflies has ever been.

The Monarch Butterfly (Danaus plexippus) is a small orange-and-black insect that flies three thousand miles to a forest it has never seen. Earth Day is a useful occasion to mention this — the migration is among the longest seasonal movements of any insect on Earth, and most people will go their whole lives without watching it.

In late summer, somewhere in Ontario or New England, a Monarch hatches from a milkweed leaf. It is the fourth generation of the year. Its parents lived six weeks. Its grandparents lived six weeks. This one will live eight months.

This is the Methuselah generation. The flight south begins in August. By October it has crossed the Great Lakes, the prairie, the Rio Grande, the Sierra Madre Oriental. In November it arrives at a grove of oyamel fir in Michoacán, ten thousand feet above sea level — the same dozen mountaintops, every year, where its great-great-grandparents wintered the year before. None of these butterflies has ever been here. None of them has ever met one that has.

How they navigate is still partly an open question. The insect uses the angle of the sun, corrected against an internal circadian clock in its antennae. It uses the Earth's magnetic field. It uses, possibly, the polarization of light. None of these by itself explains the precision.

The oyamel fir (Abies religiosa) grows nowhere on Earth except a band of high-elevation slopes in central Mexico. The microclimate inside the grove holds the monarchs in a careful suspension — cool enough that they do not burn through their fat reserves, warm enough that they do not freeze. A few degrees in either direction and the system collapses. The trees, in this sense, are part of the species.

Inside the groves, monarchs cluster on trunks and branches in the millions. The weight of them bends the boughs. The trees go orange. The forest, in winter, sounds like rain — the soft constant rasp of wings.

In March they fly back north. They mate on the way. The females lay eggs on milkweed in northern Mexico and Texas, then die. Their offspring continue north. Their grandchildren reach the Midwest. Their great-grandchildren — the next Methuselah generation — will be born in late summer and will fly the whole arc south, to a grove they have never seen.

Milkweed (Asclepias) is the only plant Monarch caterpillars eat. The plant is mildly toxic; the caterpillar takes the toxins into its own body and keeps them. The orange of the adult is aposematic — the warning color of an insect that tastes terrible to a bird. Without milkweed, no monarchs. The decline of milkweed across North American cropland in the last forty years is the simplest explanation for what has happened to the population.

Conservationists measure the eastern overwintering not in individuals but in hectares of occupied forest. In 1996 the count was eighteen hectares. In 2024 it was 1.79.

The pocket art is the adult — orange forewings veined in black, a band of white spots along the margin, the proboscis curled, the wings at rest. Painted by hand at the size of the pocket, in Procreate, by the studio.

Stand in an oyamel grove in February. The butterflies are quiet on the trunks. Then the sun breaks through, and the forest opens its wings.
